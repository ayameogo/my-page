CS 01
ServiceTitan · 2025

From Assist to Atlas

100% of product teams on one AI framework. +84% adoption growth. Agentic UX with human approval gates built in from day one.
AI Strategy Agentic UX
+

The Challenge

AI capabilities were emerging independently across ServiceTitan's product teams — creating real risk of fragmented experiences, inconsistent patterns, and duplicated work. There was no shared model for how AI should interact with users, how transparency should be handled, or how trust should be built at the user level.

The deeper problem: building individual AI features is relatively easy. Building a framework that scales across 7 product domains, earns user trust, and holds quality at every touchpoint — that's a design infrastructure problem.

The Approach

Design led a structured evolution across four phases. Phase 1 established the platform infrastructure (Titan Intelligence). Phase 2 — the design-led breakthrough — delivered the Assist Framework: reusable interaction patterns (conversational prompts, insight cards, recommended actions, confirmations) delivered for the company hackathon within two months.

Phase 3 introduced Atlas — a named AI assistant that personifies intelligence for users. The key insight validated by user research: "When it doesn't have a name, it's not a natural interaction." Framework-first thinking prevented fragmentation. Phase 4 maps the north star: proactive agentic experiences with human approval always in the loop.

CS 02
ServiceTitan · 2024

Global Search

78K users. 92% click-through. Secured CTO investment by reframing a UX problem as platform infrastructure — and the retrieval layer for AI agents.
Platform Strategy Search
+

The Challenge

ServiceTitan's search reflected database architecture, not user intent. Users were required to select an object type before searching — forced to think like the database. Research revealed why this was broken: 67% search for invoices after opening a customer; 62% after opening a job. Users aren't searching for objects — they're searching for outcomes.

Customer verbatim: "Search functionality is sub par" and "Searching for customers and appointments is very time consuming." The model was fundamentally wrong.

The Approach

Design reframed the problem as a platform investment, not a UI refresh. The solution: unified global search with cross-entity results, hierarchical grouping, recently viewed shortcuts, typo tolerance, rich result previews, and quick action affordances — powered by Elasticsearch.

Strategic expansion: search became the retrieval layer for Atlas agents. Without robust, trustworthy search, agentic systems can't function — hallucination risk rises. Design leadership secured CTO funding by framing search as platform capability, not a feature request. That reframing was the pivotal move.

CS 03
ServiceTitan · 2021–Present

Scaling a Global Design Org

97% team engagement. 60% women in design leadership. ~736h annual efficiency gained by rewiring how the org works — not just what tools it uses.
Org Design Leadership
+

The Challenge

The mandate at ServiceTitan: build a world-class design organization capable of operating at public-company standards while maintaining the speed and craft of a high-growth SaaS product. The org needed to span Product Design, UX Research, Design Systems, Content Design, Visual Design, DesignOps, and Agent Experience — across multiple time zones with major teams in Los Angeles and Armenia.

IPO readiness added a layer of governance complexity: investor scrutiny, predictable execution requirements, and the need to demonstrate operating discipline at every level of the org simultaneously.

The Approach

Built the org with a clear operating model: a 1:10 design-to-engineering ratio, quarterly OKR cadence, structured review and governance frameworks, and AI fluency embedded into leveling expectations. Director-level leadership was developed internally — a deliberate signal about talent investment.

The AI transformation of the org itself was equally important. Designers moved from specification writers to production contributors: 100% Figma Make adoption, 100% Cursor adoption, AI Guild training cohorts, 25% of UXD tickets AI-assisted. The org didn't just use AI — it rewired how design works. ~736 hours of annual efficiency gain documented.

CS 04
LegalZoom · 2015–2021

LZRED — The LegalZoom Redesign

Six-year platform transformation. $10B+ valuation at IPO. End-to-end redesign of products, brand, and operating model — delivered on time and ahead of public markets scrutiny.
IPO Platform Redesign
+

The Challenge

By late 2019, LegalZoom was carrying significant platform debt. 58% of traffic was mobile, but the bounce rate on content pages hit 73% — and checkout abandonment was high. The product lacked pricing transparency, had too many upsells, and the site reflected years of accumulated inconsistency: multiple checkout flows to maintain, outdated visual language, and a user experience that had drifted far from what customers actually needed.

Two deadlines made this non-negotiable: new LegalZoom commercials were set to launch in February 2021 — and the IPO was around the corner in spring 2021. The redesign had to ship with both.

The Approach

Design led the pitch internally. A small cross-functional team — 3 designers, a Director of Product Design, a Product Marketer, and a PM — spent three weeks in a conference room running quick provocations anchored in existing research: customer journeys, established personas, prior usability testing. The board approved the end-to-end prototype by May.

Execution spanned four phases over 12 months. The scope was significant: new visual identity and brand architecture, a new design system (Glo) built on atomic principles, 100+ product and pricing pages, 8,000+ content pages, five major questionnaires, a unified global checkout (consolidating five separate flows into one), and a new email design system. 80% of the design and engineering team was dedicated to LZRED — while the rest of the business kept moving.

COVID hit mid-project. Operations moved fully virtual. Engineers were borrowed from other initiatives and hired offshore to protect the timeline. Design decisions were made intentionally and quickly — testing on key pages only, avoiding major copy changes that could affect package mix, keeping the IA stable to de-risk delivery. Every tradeoff was deliberate.
